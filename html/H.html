The Higgs boson

The Higgs Boson, also known colloquially as the "God Particle" is the particle in that proves the existence of the Higgs Field, which is theorized to be the mechanism by which everything in the universe gets mass. It is the smallest possible excitation of the Higgs field, and can be though of as the "mediator" between the Higgs field and the rest of the universe. CERN has observed what they believe to be a Higgs Boson with mass of about 125 GeV, and this observation has been tentatively confirmed, but it is still unclear that this is the Higgs Boson found in the standard model, or that it is the only Higgs boson.

History of the Higgs boson

The Higgs Boson is named for Peter Higgs, who wrote a paper which proposed a massive, spin-zero boson that would allow for a consistent relativistic field theory where both massless and massive particles could carry force. This "Higgs Boson", if found, would be a huge step towards a unified standard model, and as such, it became a huge goal for many supercolliders. On 14 March 2013, the LHC found what they tentatively believe to be a Higgs Boson, but further research is still being conducted to confirm and refine the results.
